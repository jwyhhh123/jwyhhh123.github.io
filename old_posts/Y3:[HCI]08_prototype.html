Sketch vs Prototype

Sketching

  1. exploring ideas
  2. Used in earlier in the design process
  3. Try to find the Right design

Prototype

  1. testing ideas,
  2. getting feedbacks
  3. focuses down to certain aspects of design.
  4. interactive
  5. different stages of process, different purposes

Ptototype is about answering questions, subset of look and feel and subset of functionality, deal with specific issues you have

Reason to prototype

  • Evaluation and feedback
  • helps with cross team communication
  • encourage reflection
  • helps choose between alternatives

What to prototype

  1. Technical issues
  2. Work flow and task design
  3. environment/ experience
  4. screen layouts and information display
  5. difficult, controversial, critical areas

types of prototypes

Works-Like/Looks-like Prototypes
Works-like prototype:

  • focus on functionality
  • technolohical proof of concept
  • Typically earlier stage

Looks-like prototypes

  • Industrial/visual design exploration
  • What it looks, feels like, how well it communicates its functionality
  • sample content simulations
  • typically later stage

Low Fidelity vs High fidelity
1] Lo-Fi: quick and ready, rough and simple

  1. Uses a medium which is unlike the final medium e.g. paper, cardboard
  2. Quick, cheap & easy to modify
  3. Should be flexible & encourage exploration
  4. sketches of screens, task sequences, ‘Post-it’ notes, ‘Wizard-of-Oz’
  5. Used more to explore than evaluate with users

Examples of Lo-Fi:

  1. paper,
  2. card-based
  3. Wire-framing
  4. powerpoint
  5. HTML
  6. Interface building toolkits (not quick)
  7. simulators

Which prototype method to choose

Depends on:

  • What are you designing?
  • What are the important aspects of your design
    • Appearance e.g. size, colour, weight, shape feel, sound
    • Functionality e.g. how the system functions and the users
    needs
    • Interactivity e.g. input behaviour, output behaviour,
    feedback, information
    • Spatial structure e.g. the arrangement of the interface or
    information and the relationships between
    • Data e.g. size, type, privacy, use, organisation.
  • Depends on your time, resources and skills
