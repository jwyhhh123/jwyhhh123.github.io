Problems in Design

  • Designers want to select a solution approach quickly, which may lead to premature commitment to their first design ideas
  • Designers attempt to quickly simplify the problem space with external constraints, such as the reuse of familiar solutions
  • Designers are intent on elaborating their current design proposal, resulting in inadequate analysis of other ideas or alternatives

Hill climbing

Stages: right design and design right

  1. Searching the design space as wide as possible
  2. improve it as much as possible by exploring variations

Design alternatives

the best way to get a good idea, is to get lots of ideas” (Linus Pauling)
part of the process: dropping bad ideas.

Sketching is a tool to find alternatives

  • Early in the design process it
  • usual to generate many sketches a day
  • each might explore a different concept

Why sketch:

  • To explore alternatives and quickly try them out
  • They should suggest and explore, rather than confirm
  • They can stimulate your imagination
  • They are not final – quick and cheap to make and can be thrown away if rubbish
  • low commitment

NOTES:

  • It’s fine if they look rough, sketchy!
  • Use minimal detail, leave space for imagination

What to sketch

  1. Alternatives
  2. Details
  3. Annotate drawings

can use other tools not only pens and papers

Iterate: Explore the space, refine, detail

Sketch are for:

  • failure
  • coming up with ideas
  • force you to visualize how ideas come together
  • experimenting
  • communicate ideas
  • find the right design

Design goals

We are trying to design an interactive product for our users that they will want
to use – so along with meeting our functional requirements we want something:

  • That will be usable
  • Will give the users a great user experience

Usability goals

  • Effective to use (effectiveness: does it achieve what users wanted)
  • Efficient to use (efficiency: reach their goal quickly)
  • Safe to use (safety)
  • Having good utility (utility: does it fulfills the functions for users to achieve their goals)
  • Easy to learn (learnability)
  • Easy to remember how to use (memorability)

Experience goals: for example enjoyable, entertaining

credible: could you believe it, does it provide with high accuracy.

Design principles

Norman’s 6 Design Principles

  1. Visibility: tradeoff between visibility ofprimary and secondary functions
  2. Feedback
  3. Constraints: limit the possible actions
  4. Mapping: how controls relate to their effects in the world
  5. Consistency: similar operations and similar elements (rules easier to learn)
  6. Affordance: provide clues to their functions

Shneiderman’s 8 Golden Rules

  1. Strive for consistency
  2. Cater to universal usability(e.g. color blind users)
  3. Offer informative feedback
  4. Design dialogs to yield closure
  5. Offer error prevention and simple error handling
  6. Permit easy reversal of actions
  7. Support internal locus of control (feedbacks to give users control)
  8. Reduce short-term memory load

Nielsen’s 10 Heuristics (more for evaluation)

  1. Visibility of system status
  2. Match between system and real world
  3. User control and freedom
  4. Consistency and standards
  5. Error prevention
  6. Recognition over recall
  7. Flexibility and efficiency of use
  8. Aesthetic and minimalist design
  9. Help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors
    10.Help and documentation

All design depends on the user’s…
• skills and knowledge
• education and training
• roles and responsibilities
• motivation and goals
• individual attributes: age, physical abilities, cultural
• background, personality, etc

Design for different levels of expertise

  • novice users
  • restrict vocabulary to simple, necessary terms
  • minimal number of actions to achieve task
  • provide feedback
  • Intermediate users
  • use consistent structure and terminology for menus
  • emphasize recognition over recall
  • reference materials (on-line or printed)
  • Expert users
